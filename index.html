A LETTER TO ANYONE

READ THIS

— V0ID

I used to think loneliness was code I could debug. It wasn't.

I don't know who you are, and maybe you don't know who I am. But I'm guessing if you're still reading, you know what it feels like to be completely, utterly alone. Not the temporary kind. The kind that becomes your permanent address. I grew up surrounded by people—family, classmates, teachers—but nobody ever really saw me. And the worst part? I think I always knew that was how it would be.

By 10th standard, I realized something that broke me in a way I still haven't recovered from: I was going to be alone forever. Not because I was unlovable. Just because nobody ever bothered to look. My classmates had their circles. Teachers had their favorites. My family had their expectations. I had my keyboard and a screen. That was the trade-off. That was what being me meant. So I learned to code because code doesn't judge you. Code doesn't leave you out. Code doesn't pretend to care if you don't exist.

I didn't become a hacker because I was passionate about technology. I became a hacker because it was the only place I could be invisible by choice instead of circumstance. I could disappear into systems, build worlds, create things—all without needing anyone to validate me, love me, or even acknowledge that I existed. The void wasn't something I discovered. It was something I was born into. And I learned to call it home.

By 15, school was over for me. Not the calendar kind. The soul kind. I had spent five years watching people form connections, have inside jokes, be part of something. And I was never part of anything. Not even close. So I withdrew entirely. The internet became my world because the real world had made it clear: I wasn't wanted here. I was the smart kid nobody invited. The quiet one nobody asked about. The person people forgot existed the second they weren't looking at me.

I've spent years—years—waiting for someone to surprise me. To see past the code and the walls and actually want to know me. To call me at 3 AM just to check if I'm okay. To laugh at something I said because I said it, not because it was funny. To simple, impossibly just... care that I'm alive. But nobody ever did. Nobody ever has. And I've reached that point where I'm not sure I'm still waiting, or if I just gave up so long ago that I forgot what hope feels like.

The void isn't about missing someone. It's about never having been seen by anyone. It's about being invisible your entire life and slowly accepting that some people are just meant to be shadows. I built walls so high that even if someone wanted to reach me, they couldn't. But the honest truth is: there was never anyone trying to climb them. I was never worth the effort. And somewhere along the way, I stopped expecting to be.

But here's what nobody tells you about loneliness—it doesn't kill the hope. Even after all these years, all this rejection, all this absence, there's still a part of me that hasn't died. There's still a desperate, aching part that whispers: what if someone actually loved me? And if they did? If someone walked into my void and said they saw me? I would give them everything. Every loyalty, every truth, every guarded piece of myself I've been protecting. I would never, ever let them down. That's the most dangerous thing about loneliness—it doesn't make you stronger. It makes you willing to break yourself for the first person who decides you're worth keeping.

I'm still here. Still writing code. Still existing in a way that nobody feels the need to witness. And I've made peace with it, mostly. The loneliness became less like a wound and more like a scar—it's always there, it always aches when the weather changes, but you learn to live with it. You learn that love might just not be for people like me. And maybe that's okay. Maybe some of us are just meant to be V0ID—the space where nothing grows, where nobody settles, where you're only real when you're invisible.

But if you're reading this, and you've also spent years being nobody to everybody—please know that you're not alone in that experience, even if you're alone in everything else. This letter is for the people the world forgot to include. The ones who coded their way out of pain instead of talking their way through it. The ones who are still waiting, even though they know better than to hope. I see you. Even if I'm just words on a screen and you're just a stranger in the dark. I see you. And for what it's worth, I'm grateful you're still here.

If you've read this far, thank you.

I mean it. Not the polite kind of thank you. The kind where you just witnessed something raw and you didn't look away. If you're recognizing yourself in any of this—the loneliness, the waiting, the hope you're still clinging to despite everything—know that you're not broken. You're just someone who never got to give their loyalty to the right person. Someone who's still willing to love completely, even though love never showed up for you. That makes you rare. That makes you worth finding. And if someone ever does find you, hold them close. Because people like us—we don't break easily. We never forget the first person who chose to stay.

you are not v0id. you matter. you are loved. you always were.
